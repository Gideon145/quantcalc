How to Calculate Liquidation Price in Crypto

Understand leverage mechanics and avoid getting liquidated

What is Liquidation Price?

Liquidation price is the price level at which your leveraged position is automatically closed by the exchange to prevent your account balance from going negative. When trading with leverage on perpetual futures, you're borrowing capital from the exchange. If the market moves against you enough that your collateral can't cover potential losses, the exchange liquidates your position to protect itself.

Getting liquidated means you lose 100% of the collateral you put up for that trade. On a $1,000 position with 10x leverage, you only posted $100 as collateral—but if liquidated, that entire $100 is gone. Understanding your liquidation price before entering a trade is critical for survival in leveraged trading.

How Leverage Affects Liquidation

Leverage multiplies both your potential gains and your liquidation risk. The higher your leverage, the closer your liquidation price is to your entry:

  • 1x leverage (spot): No liquidation risk—you own the asset
  • 2x leverage: Liquidated if price moves 50% against you
  • 5x leverage: Liquidated if price moves 20% against you
  • 10x leverage: Liquidated if price moves 10% against you
  • 20x leverage: Liquidated if price moves 5% against you
  • 50x leverage: Liquidated if price moves 2% against you

This is why high leverage is so dangerous in volatile crypto markets. A 5% price swing can happen in minutes, wiping out 50x leveraged positions before traders can react.

The Liquidation Price Formula

The basic formula for calculating liquidation price depends on whether you're long or short:

For Long Positions:
Liquidation Price = Entry Price × (1 - 1/Leverage)

For Short Positions:
Liquidation Price = Entry Price × (1 + 1/Leverage)

This is a simplified formula. Real exchanges use more complex calculations that include maintenance margin, funding rates, and trading fees. But this formula gives you the approximate liquidation level.

Example 1: Long Position with 10x Leverage

Scenario: Buying SOL with 10x Leverage

You open a long position on SOL:

  • Entry Price: $100
  • Position Size: $10,000 (10 SOL worth)
  • Leverage: 10x
  • Collateral Posted: $1,000 (10% of position)
Liquidation Price = $100 × (1 - 1/10)
Liquidation Price = $100 × (1 - 0.10)
Liquidation Price = $100 × 0.90
Liquidation Price = $90

If SOL drops to $90, you'll be liquidated and lose your entire $1,000 collateral. This is only a 10% price move, but because of 10x leverage, it wipes out 100% of your margin.

Example 2: Short Position with 5x Leverage

Scenario: Shorting a Meme Coin

You think a token is overvalued and want to short it:

  • Entry Price: $1.00
  • Position Size: $5,000
  • Leverage: 5x
  • Collateral Posted: $1,000
Liquidation Price = $1.00 × (1 + 1/5)
Liquidation Price = $1.00 × (1 + 0.20)
Liquidation Price = $1.00 × 1.20
Liquidation Price = $1.20

If the token pumps to $1.20 (only a 20% move), you're liquidated. Short squeezes in crypto can easily produce 50-100% pumps in hours, destroying short positions instantly.

Example 3: Comparing Different Leverage Levels

Same Entry, Different Leverage

All scenarios: Long SOL at $100 entry price

2x Leverage:

Liquidation: $100 × (1 - 1/2) = $50
Distance to liquidation: 50%

5x Leverage:

Liquidation: $100 × (1 - 1/5) = $80
Distance to liquidation: 20%

20x Leverage:

Liquidation: $100 × (1 - 1/20) = $95

Liquidation: $100 × (1 - 1/50) = $98
Distance to liquidation: 2%

Notice how 50x leverage gives you only $2 of room before liquidation. One market maker stop-hunt and you're done.

Why Traders Get Liquidated

  • Using too much leverage: 20x+ leverage in crypto is gambling, not trading
  • Not monitoring positions: Falling asleep with 10x leverage during volatile hours
  • Ignoring funding rates: High funding rates slowly drain your margin, moving liquidation price closer
  • No stop loss: Hoping the market will reverse instead of cutting losses early
  • Overleveraging entire account: One position uses 80% of margin, leaving no buffer
  • Cascading liquidations: Your liquidation triggers others, causing flash crashes
  • Not accounting for fees: Trading fees reduce effective margin, lowering liquidation threshold

Hidden Factors That Move Liquidation Price

Your Real Liquidation Price Changes Over Time

The simple formula gives you the initial liquidation price, but several factors move it during your trade:

  • Funding rates: If you're long and funding is positive, you pay fees every 8 hours, reducing your margin
  • Realized PnL from other positions: Profits from other trades increase your margin, losses decrease it
  • Maintenance margin requirements: Exchanges increase margin requirements during high volatility
  • Unrealized losses from correlated positions: If you have multiple leveraged positions moving against you
  • Liquidation engine delays: During extreme volatility, you might be liquidated past your theoretical liquidation price

Risk Management for Leveraged Trading

Professional traders use these strategies to avoid liquidation:

Safe Leverage Approach

  • Maximum 5x leverage: Even in strong setups, never exceed 5x
  • Use 30-50% of available margin: If you have $10,000, only use $3-5k for positions
  • Set stop loss 30-40% away from liquidation: Don't let positions approach liquidation
  • Calculate liquidation before entering: Know exactly where you'll be liquidated
  • Close positions before sleep: Never hold high-leverage overnight
  • Add margin when approaching liquidation: But only if thesis is still valid

Cross Margin vs Isolated Margin

Most exchanges offer two margin modes that affect how liquidation works:

Isolated Margin:

  • Each position has its own collateral pool
  • Liquidation of one position doesn't affect others
  • Liquidation price is fixed based on that position's margin
  • Safer for managing risk—one bad trade doesn't kill your account

Cross Margin:

  • All positions share your entire account balance as collateral
  • Profits from one trade support losses in another
  • Lower risk of liquidation per position
  • Higher risk of total account liquidation if multiple positions fail

For most traders, isolated margin is safer. It forces you to size positions appropriately and prevents one catastrophic trade from destroying your entire account.

Real-World Liquidation Scenarios

The Flash Crash Liquidation

January 2025: SOL dumps 15% in 5 minutes due to macro news

  • Trader A: 10x long SOL at $100, liquidation at $90 → Liquidated at $88 (slippage)
  • Trader B: 5x long SOL at $100, liquidation at $80 → Survived, position at -12.5% unrealized
  • Trader C: 20x long SOL at $100, liquidation at $95 → Liquidated immediately

During flash crashes, liquidation engines execute orders into low liquidity, often liquidating you at worse prices than your theoretical liquidation level. Always add 2-5% buffer to your calculated liquidation price.

How to Calculate Safe Position Size

Instead of thinking "how much leverage should I use?", think "where do I want my liquidation price?"

Step 1: Identify your invalidation point (where trade thesis is wrong)
Step 2: Calculate required leverage to set liquidation there
Step 3: Calculate position size based on that leverage

Example:
Entry: $100, Invalidation: $85 (15% away)
Required Leverage: 1 / 0.15 = 6.67x
Use 5x leverage to stay safe (below max)
Liquidation: $100 × (1 - 1/5) = $80
Buffer: 5% below invalidation point ✓

This approach sizes leverage based on your trade idea, not arbitrary numbers. Use a position size calculator to determine exact sizing based on your risk tolerance.

Common Liquidation Mistakes

  • Revenge trading after liquidation: Immediately re-entering with higher leverage to "make it back"
  • Not accounting for spread: Bid-ask spread can trigger liquidation earlier than calculated
  • Holding through major events: FOMC, CPI, or protocol announcements with high leverage
  • Assuming exchanges protect you: Exchange liquidation engines prioritize platform solvency, not your position
  • Not using stop losses: Relying on liquidation as your stop loss means you lose 100% instead of taking a smaller loss
  • Pyramiding into losing positions: Adding to losers pushes your average entry toward liquidation
